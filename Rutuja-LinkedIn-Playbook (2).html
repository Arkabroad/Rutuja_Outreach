Day 1 Professional Insight
Every design I start begins with the site. Not the brief. Not the precedents. The actual ground. Before I draw a single line, I spend time understanding what the space already is: its ecology, its movement patterns, who uses it and how, what the surrounding context is asking for. That habit came from my time as a junior architect in India, where I learned quickly that designs which ignore their context get rejected. By government authorities. By clients. By the people who actually have to live or move through the space. It shaped everything I did in my Master of Urban Design at the University of Sydney, from precinct planning proposals to a research paper on enhancing biodiversity in compact cities. Melbourne has an interesting challenge with this right now. Dense development is happening fast. The 2026 Australian Urban Design Awards recognised street trees and green infrastructure as essential city infrastructure, not optional additions. That framing is exactly right. The best urban design does not add green space after the hard work is done. It starts there. What has been the most useful thing you have learned about a site before starting a design?
Day 5 Personal Truth
There is a project in India that I designed from a blank page. It got approved by the client. Then it went through government sanction. Now it is being built. I was an intern at the time. That is not the normal outcome for an intern. I got the project because I had done the site analysis more thoroughly than anyone else on the team. I understood the client's brief better than the brief itself stated. And I had a clear idea of what the design needed to be, not just what would look good in a portfolio. When it got sanctioned by the planning authority, I thought the feeling would be the approval itself. It wasn't. The feeling was the day I found out it was actually under construction. That a real place in the world exists because of something I designed. That is the part of architecture nobody tells you about before you start. What was the moment that confirmed for you that design was the right direction?
Day 10 Market Observation
Melbourne is building at pace right now. New precincts, transport corridors, infill housing, public realm projects. The 2026 Australian Urban Design Awards had over 80 entries across built outcomes, research, and strategic planning. Here is what I notice as someone who studied urban design in Sydney and is now based in Melbourne: the projects being recognised are not the ones that look most impressive on paper. They are the ones that treated streets, trees, and biodiversity as infrastructure from the start, not as additions once the engineering was done. That shift in how the industry thinks about green infrastructure is exactly what my research on urban green spaces in compact cities was about. Not parks added to the edge of a precinct. Green space woven into the structure of how a place works. Melbourne is building the right things in many places. The question being asked in the best projects is whether it is building them the right way. What urban design project in Melbourne has impressed you most recently and why?
